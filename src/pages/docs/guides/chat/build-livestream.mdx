## Architecting your chat rooms: UX first, scale always

The most important architectural decision is how you structure your chat rooms. With Ably, you are not limited by technology—only by the user experience you want to deliver.

- **Single massive room:** Ably supports millions of users in a single room, with no sharding required. This is ideal for global events or streams where everyone participates together.
- **Many concurrent rooms:** You can run thousands of rooms in parallel, each with their own audience and message flow. Ably's infrastructure scales horizontally, so you never hit a ceiling.

**Key consideration:**
- What experience do you want for your users? Should everyone see the same messages, or do you need to segment by language, region, or topic?
- Do you need to support both public and private chat?

Ably gives you the flexibility to design for your users, not your infrastructure.

## Message throughput and rate management

Livestream chat is defined by high message rates and unpredictable spikes. Ably is engineered to handle these loads without degradation.

- **Proven at scale:** Ably delivers over 500 million messages per day for customers, with individual rooms supporting over 1 million concurrent users.
- **Server-side batching:** Reduce costs and network overhead by grouping messages before delivery. For example, batching messages over a 1–2 second interval can reduce message costs by up to 90% in high-traffic streams.

## Moderation: Protecting your community

Effective moderation is essential for livestream chat. Ably supports both inline (before-publish) and after-publish moderation workflows.

- **After-publish moderation:** Messages appear instantly, then are removed if flagged. This minimises latency and is suitable for high-velocity chats.
- **Before-publish moderation:** Messages are held until approved. Use this for sensitive streams where content control is paramount.
- **AI integration:** Ably's APIs make it easy to integrate with third-party moderation tools or custom AI models.
